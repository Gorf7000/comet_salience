1908 III (1908c). 
Discovered at the Des Moines (Iowa) University 
Observatory on 1 Sept. by Morehouse on a photograph showing the comet 
as a 9™ object with a long tail. 
Discovered independently by Borrelly 
(Marseilles) on 3 Sept. 
One of the remarkable comets of the 20th century that played a particular 
role in the evolution of our knowledge of the processes in cometary heads 
and tails. 
Located in Camelopardus; moved through Cassiopeia, Cepheus 
(September), Cygnus, Lyra (October), Hercules, Aquila (November), 
Scutum and Sagittarius (December) approaching conjunction with the Sun. 
At the northern observatories, observed till 14 Dec. (Rome and Lick Obs.); 
in the southern hemisphere, observed from 22 Jan. 1909. 
Passed through 
Corona Australis, Telescopium (February 1909), Pavo, Apus, Chamaeleon 
(March), Carina, Vela (April) and Pyxis (May). 
Favorable celestial position and considerable brightness of the comet 
enabled numerous observers to obtain prolonged series of photometric, 
photographic and spectrographic observations. 
The most important were 
the estimates of intrinsic and nuclear brightness and size of head and tail 
undertaken by Holetschek (Vienna) 13 Sept. to 5 Dec., Nijland (Utrecht,
